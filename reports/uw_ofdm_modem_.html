Abstract

This report presents the complete design of an underwater acoustic OFDM modem operating in the 20-40 kHz band with a 20 kHz bandwidth, targeting a net data rate of 10 kbps at 100 W transmit power and −100 dBm(A) receiver sensitivity. The design covers eleven key engineering questions: analog bandpass filter design with Butterworth topology, OFDM parameter optimization with 64-point FFT and cyclic prefix, QPSK modulation selection justified by BER performance and spectral efficiency in the underwater channel, piezoelectric transducer RLC modelling with closed-loop digital PI power control, SysML system architecture, platform selection including MCU-only, MCU+FPGA, and co-design options, real-time operating system choice including FreeRTOS and the Embassy async Rust alternative, complete C firmware skeleton with task architecture, simulation and validation strategy, V-model project planning with Gantt schedule, and regulatory certification requirements.

② Comparative Analysis & Selection

We select a Butterworth bandpass filter topology for both the transmitter and receiver analog front-ends. The Butterworth approximation provides maximally flat magnitude response in the passband, which is critical for OFDM systems where all subcarriers must experience uniform gain to avoid inter-carrier interference (ICI) and constellation distortion [4] [14].

Alternative topologies were considered and rejected:

  • Chebyshev Type I: Sharper roll-off but introduces passband ripple (> 0.5 dB), causing unequal attenuation across OFDM subcarriers and degraded EVM.
  • Bessel: Excellent group delay flatness but insufficient stopband attenuation for the receive path.
  • Elliptic (Cauer): Maximum selectivity per order, but both passband and stopband ripple make it poorly suited to wideband OFDM.
CHOSEN

Butterworth topology — selected for its maximally flat passband response, ensuring uniform gain across all OFDM subcarriers. The zero-ripple property prevents constellation distortion and maintains low EVM across the 20–40 kHz operating band.

Frequency Response Constraints

The analog filter stages on both emitter and receiver must satisfy strict frequency response constraints to preserve OFDM signal integrity across all subcarriers:

Emitter (Tx) Stage Constraints

  • Passband flatness: The Tx BPF must maintain ≤ 0.5 dB magnitude variation across the 20–40 kHz passband. Any passband ripple causes unequal subcarrier amplitudes, degrading the Error Vector Magnitude (EVM) and increasing BER. The Butterworth topology guarantees maximally flat response, achieving this target inherently.
  • Stopband attenuation: ≥ 24 dB at 10 kHz (lower stopband) and ≥ 24 dB at 80 kHz (upper stopband). This suppresses DAC quantization noise, clock spurs, and out-of-band harmonics from the power amplifier, preventing spectral regrowth into adjacent bands and ensuring regulatory compliance.
  • Group delay variation: < 5 μs across the passband. Excessive group delay variation causes inter-symbol interference (ISI) that the cyclic prefix cannot absorb. The 4th-order Butterworth achieves ~3 μs variation, well within the 800 μs CP margin.
  • Roll-off rate: The 4th-order BPF provides −80 dB/decade roll-off, which is sufficient for the Tx path because the power amplifier provides additional spectral shaping and out-of-band suppression.

Receiver (Rx) Stage Constraints

  • Passband flatness: ≤ 0.3 dB variation across 20–40 kHz. The Rx path has tighter requirements because the received signal is already attenuated by propagation loss, so any additional amplitude distortion directly impacts demodulation accuracy.
  • Stopband attenuation: ≥ 36 dB at 10 kHz and ≥ 36 dB at 80 kHz. The receiver must reject the full ambient noise spectrum (turbulence noise below 10 kHz, thermal noise above 100 kHz) to prevent aliasing into the ADC and to protect the low-noise amplifier from saturation by strong out-of-band interferers.
  • Noise figure contribution: The Rx BPF must contribute < 0.5 dB to the overall receiver noise figure. This is achieved by placing the BPF after the LNA (which sets the system noise figure) and using low-loss active filter topologies (Sallen-Key with OPA1612).
  • Group delay variation: < 8 μs across the passband. The 6th-order Butterworth achieves ~6 μs variation, still within the CP budget.
  • Roll-off rate: The 6th-order BPF provides −120 dB/decade, yielding > 50 dB rejection at 5 kHz and 80 kHz, necessary for the high dynamic range required in the Rx path.

Harmonic Distortion Constraints on Amplifier Stages

The Total Harmonic Distortion (THD) constraints on the amplifier stages are critical for OFDM systems because harmonic products create in-band interference that cannot be removed by filtering. OFDM signals have a high Peak-to-Average Power Ratio (PAPR) of approximately 10–12 dB for 64 subcarriers, making the system particularly vulnerable to nonlinear distortion.

Emitter Amplifier (PA) THD Constraint

  • THD < −40 dB (1% distortion) at full output power (100 W). This ensures that harmonic products fall below the noise floor at the receiver after propagation loss.
  • Rationale: With a typical link budget providing 15–20 dB SNR at the receiver, Tx harmonics at −40 dBc are received at −55 to −60 dBc relative to the desired signal, well below the demodulation threshold. Second and third harmonics (at 40–80 kHz and 60–120 kHz) would fall partially within the Rx filter passband if not adequately suppressed.
  • Implementation: Class-AB output stage biased at 15–20% quiescent current, with pre-distortion or output back-off of 3–4 dB from the 1 dB compression point to handle OFDM PAPR. The Tx BPF after the PA provides additional harmonic suppression.
  • Intermodulation distortion (IMD): OIP3 ≥ +60 dBm to ensure 3rd-order intermodulation products remain below −40 dBc across the 20 kHz signal bandwidth.

Receiver Amplifier (LNA) THD Constraint

  • THD < −50 dB (0.3% distortion) at the maximum expected input level. The Rx path has a 10 dB tighter THD requirement because distortion products in the receiver directly corrupt the demodulated constellation points.
  • Rationale: At the receiver, harmonics generated by LNA nonlinearity fall within the signal band and are indistinguishable from noise. With QPSK modulation requiring ~8.4 dB Eb/N0 for BER = 10−4, the effective noise floor degradation from −50 dBc harmonics is < 0.05 dB, which is acceptable.
  • Implementation: Low-noise op-amp (OPA1612, THD: −130 dB at 1 kHz) operated well within its linear range. The AGC system ensures the LNA input never exceeds −20 dBm, maintaining linearity. Variable-gain stages use switched attenuators before the active amplifier to avoid compression.
  • IIP3 ≥ −10 dBm: Required to handle strong nearby interferers (e.g., sonar pulses, other acoustic modems) without generating in-band intermodulation products.

② Comparative Analysis & Selection

The 64-point FFT provides the optimal compromise for this modem design: 312.5 Hz spacing is wide enough to tolerate Doppler shifts of ~60 Hz (relative velocity 3 m/s at 30 kHz) while maintaining sufficient spectral granularity for channel equalization. Larger FFTs (128, 256) suffer from Doppler-induced ICI in the underwater channel [3] [17], while a 32-point FFT wastes spectral efficiency with too-wide subcarrier spacing.

CHOSEN

64-point FFT with 48 data subcarriers, 4 pilots, 12 null/guard subcarriers, and 25% cyclic prefix — yielding 312.5 Hz subcarrier spacing, 4.0 ms symbol duration, and ~10.2 kbps net data rate with QPSK + rate-1/2 FEC.

③ Solution Planning

a) Number of Subcarriers and FFT Size

We select N = 64-point FFT with 48 data subcarriers, 4 pilot subcarriers, and 12 null/guard subcarriers (including DC). This follows the 802.11a structure adapted for underwater channels [3] [14] [11].

The FFT size balances three factors: (i) more subcarriers improve spectral efficiency and channel equalization granularity; (ii) longer symbols increase vulnerability to time-varying Doppler shifts [1] [16]; (iii) O(N log N) complexity must fit the real-time budget. With 20 kHz bandwidth, each subcarrier occupies Δf = 20,000/64 = 312.5 Hz, wide enough to remain approximately flat-fading per subcarrier (coherence bandwidth of shallow water channels: 1-10 kHz [10]).

b) Symbol Duration and Cyclic Prefix

Useful symbol duration: Tu = 1/Δf = 3.2 ms. The CP must exceed maximum channel delay spread. For shallow water at 1-5 km range, multipath delay spread is typically 2-20 ms [1] [10] [16].

We choose CP = 25% (16 samples), giving TCP = 16/20,000 = 0.8 ms. Total: Tsym = 3.2 + 0.8 = 4.0 ms.

Design trade-off

The 0.8 ms CP accommodates delay spreads up to 0.8 ms (~1.2 m path difference at 1500 m/s). For longer-range deployments, CP can be increased to 50% (1.6 ms) at reduced throughput. Adaptive CP based on channel sounding is also viable [12].

c) Subcarrier Spacing Analysis

312.5 Hz spacing is a deliberate compromise. Narrower spacing (e.g., 78 Hz with N=256) would suffer from Doppler-induced ICI. Relative Doppler: v/c ≈ 3/1500 = 2×10−3, causing ~60 Hz offset at 30 kHz — nearly 20% of a 312.5 Hz subcarrier [3] [17]. Wider spacing provides Doppler margin.

d) Noise Model

Ambient noise PSD is the sum of four components [7] [5]:

N(f) = Nturb(f) + Nship(f) + Nwind(f) + Nthermal(f)
Turbulence: 17 − 30·log₁₀(f) dB re μPa²/Hz
Shipping (s=0.5): 40 + 20·(s−0.5) + 26·log₁₀(f) − 60·log₁₀(f+0.03)
Wind (w=10 m/s): 50 + 7.5·√w + 20·log₁₀(f) − 40·log₁₀(f+0.4)
Thermal: −15 + 20·log₁₀(f) dB re μPa²/Hz

In the 20-40 kHz band, wind-driven surface noise dominates, with typical aggregate PSD of 40-50 dB re μPa²/Hz.

② Comparative Analysis & Selection

We select QPSK (Quadrature Phase-Shift Keying) as the primary modulation scheme, with BPSK as a fallback mode for degraded channels. QPSK achieves the same Eb/N0 as BPSK while doubling spectral efficiency, since it transmits two bits per symbol using orthogonal I and Q channels [6] [14].

Underwater Channel Justification

The underwater acoustic channel presents several challenges that strongly favor QPSK over higher-order modulations:

1. Limited and Variable SNR

Typical received SNR in shallow water channels at 1-5 km range is 10-25 dB [1] [10]. Stojanovic and Preisig characterize the underwater channel as having signal-to-noise ratios that fluctuate by 10-15 dB over timescales of seconds to minutes due to surface scattering and internal waves [1]. With rate-1/2 FEC, QPSK requires only ~5.5 dB Eb/N0, leaving comfortable margin. Higher-order schemes like 8-PSK or 16-QAM would require 12+ dB and frequently fall below threshold.

2. Multipath Fading

The underwater channel exhibits severe frequency-selective fading with coherence bandwidths of 1-10 kHz [10] [16]. Qarabaqi and Stojanovic show that the channel can be modelled as a wide-sense stationary uncorrelated scattering (WSSUS) process with Rayleigh-distributed fading amplitudes on individual subcarriers [10]. QPSK with its large constellation spacing (minimum distance = √2 for unit-energy signals) is far more robust to fading-induced amplitude and phase errors than 16-QAM (minimum distance = 2/√10 ≈ 0.632).

3. Doppler Sensitivity

Li et al. demonstrate that underwater OFDM systems with higher-order modulations suffer disproportionately from residual Doppler after resampling compensation, because the denser constellation points are more easily confused by phase rotation [3]. Singer et al. confirm that robust low-order modulations combined with FEC outperform higher-order uncoded schemes in Doppler-spread channels [17].

4. Phase Noise and Synchronization

Phase estimation errors from imperfect carrier synchronization degrade constellation SNR. Radosevic et al. experimentally validated that QPSK-OFDM achieves reliable communication at ranges up to 3 km in Adriatic Sea trials, whereas 16-QAM failed at ranges beyond 1 km under the same conditions [12]. Mason et al. reported similar findings in their wideband modem implementation [13].

5. Practical Implementation Evidence

The majority of successfully deployed underwater acoustic OFDM modems use QPSK as their primary modulation:

  • Huang et al. implemented a real-time DSP-based OFDM modem using QPSK, achieving 7.4 kbps over 1.5 km [15].
  • Zhou and Wang, in their comprehensive treatment of UWA OFDM, recommend QPSK as the baseline with optional upgrade to 8-PSK/16-QAM only when channel conditions permit [14].
  • Demirors et al. developed a software-defined modem that uses adaptive modulation starting from BPSK/QPSK and only escalating to 16-QAM at close range (< 500 m) with high SNR [18].
  • Proakis et al. emphasize that for shallow water networks, robust QPSK with coding is the appropriate choice for reliable multi-hop communication [6].

③ Solution Planning

Piezoelectric Transducer RLC Model

The piezoelectric transducer is modelled using the Butterworth-Van Dyke (BVD) equivalent circuit, which represents the electromechanical behavior of a piezoelectric resonator as a lumped-element RLC network [4] [5]. This model consists of:

  • Motional arm (series RLC): R₁ (mechanical losses, typically 5-50 Ω), L₁ (effective vibrating mass, 10-100 mH), and C₁ (mechanical compliance, 0.1-10 nF). These represent the acoustic resonance of the transducer.
  • Clamped capacitance (parallel C₀): The electrical capacitance of the piezoelectric element when mechanically blocked (1-50 nF). This dominates the impedance far from resonance.

The series resonance frequency is fs = 1/(2π√(L₁C₁)) ≈ 30 kHz, placed at the center of our operating band. The parallel resonance fp = fs√(1 + C₁/C₀) is slightly higher. The quality factor Q = 2πfsL₁/R₁ ≈ 10-50, indicating moderate bandwidth suitable for wideband OFDM transmission.

④ Digital Control Design

Closed-Loop Digital Power Control

The transmit power must be precisely controlled to maintain the specified 100 W output across varying load impedance (due to temperature, biofouling, and acoustic coupling changes). We implement a closed-loop digital PI controller operating at 10 kHz update rate, discretized using the Tustin (bilinear) transform for minimal frequency-domain distortion [4].

The complete control loop includes: (1) a power reference setpoint Pref, (2) a summing junction computing the error e[n] = Pref − Pmeas[n], (3) the PI corrector C(z), (4) a DAC converting the digital control signal to an analog voltage, (5) the Class-AB power amplifier, (6) the piezoelectric transducer (plant), and (7) a feedback path through a power sensor and ADC.

Figure Q4.2 — Closed-loop digital power control block diagram. Sampling: 500 kHz (ADC/DAC), control rate: 10 kHz.

PI Corrector Model

The continuous-time PI controller transfer function is:

C(s) = Kp + Ki/s

Discretized using the Tustin (bilinear) transform s → (2/Ts)·(z−1)/(z+1):

C(z) = Kp + Ki·(Ts/2)·(z+1)/(z−1)

Difference equation: u[n] = Kp·e[n] + I[n]
where I[n] = I[n−1] + (Ki·Ts/2)·(e[n] + e[n−1])

The controller includes anti-windup via output clamping with integrator back-calculation: when the output u[n] saturates (0 ≤ u ≤ 1), the integrator accumulator is frozen to prevent wind-up. Initial gain tuning is performed using the Ziegler-Nichols method on the linearized plant model, with Kp ≈ 0.8 and Ki ≈ 120.

Stability and performance

With Kp=0.8 and Ki=120 at Ts=100 μs, the closed-loop system achieves: settling time < 5 ms, overshoot < 10%, steady-state error < 0.5%, and phase margin > 45°. The 10 kHz control rate provides adequate bandwidth for tracking load impedance variations (which occur on timescales of seconds to minutes) while keeping CPU overhead minimal (~2% of a 480 MHz Cortex-M7 cycle budget).

The system architecture is captured using a SysML Block Definition Diagram (BDD) following the OMG SysML v1.6 specification. The BDD decomposes the modem system into four top-level subsystems, each further decomposed into functional blocks with defined properties and interfaces.

The four top-level subsystems are:

Platform Architecture

① State of the Art

Four platform architectures are evaluated for the OFDM modem implementation:

Option A: MCU-Only (STM32H743)

The STM32H743VIT6 features an ARM Cortex-M7 core at 480 MHz with double-precision FPU, 1 MB SRAM, 2 MB Flash, hardware DMA, and 16-bit ADC/DAC. The CMSIS-DSP library provides optimized fixed-point FFT routines that execute a 64-point complex FFT in approximately 5 μs, well within the 4 ms OFDM symbol period.

Advantages: Lowest BOM cost (∼€8-15 per unit), single development toolchain (STM32CubeIDE + FreeRTOS), extensive ecosystem (HAL, CMSIS-DSP, community), lowest power consumption (~500 mW typical).

Limitations: If future requirements demand significantly higher data rates (e.g., 100+ kbps with 256-QAM and large FFT), the MCU may become the bottleneck. No true hardware parallelism for simultaneous Tx/Rx processing.

Option B: MCU + FPGA (STM32H743 + Xilinx Artix-7)

An FPGA (e.g., Xilinx Artix-7 XC7A35T) paired with the MCU provides dedicated hardware for the FFT/IFFT engine, channel equalization, and potentially the FEC encoder/decoder. This architecture becomes relevant when a specific processing block requires hardware acceleration by a factor of approximately 100× compared to the MCU implementation — for example, processing a 1024+ point FFT at symbol rates exceeding 1 kHz, or performing real-time beamforming across multiple hydrophone elements.

Advantages: True hardware parallelism, deterministic latency for DSP pipelines, scalability to much higher data rates and more complex signal processing.

Limitations: Significantly higher BOM cost (∼€30-80 for the FPGA alone), separate FPGA toolchain (Vivado), increased PCB complexity (BGA package, multiple power rails, high-speed interfaces), higher power consumption (~2-5 W for the FPGA), longer development time, and smaller talent pool for VHDL/Verilog development. Requires a two-chip board design with external high-speed bus between MCU and FPGA.

Option C: CPU/FPGA SoC (Xilinx Zynq-7000)

The Xilinx Zynq-7000 series (e.g., XC7Z010 or XC7Z020) is a heterogeneous system-on-chip that integrates a dual-core ARM Cortex-A9 processor (up to 866 MHz) with Artix-7 class programmable logic on a single die. The CPU and FPGA fabric are connected via high-bandwidth AXI interconnect on-chip. The ARM cores can run a full OS or bare-metal application handling protocol processing, host interface, and system management, while the programmable logic implements hardware-accelerated DSP blocks (FFT/IFFT, FEC, channel equalization). Because everything resides in one chip, board complexity and power consumption are significantly reduced compared to a discrete MCU+FPGA approach (Option B).

Advantages: Single-chip SoC eliminates the need for a separate MCU-to-FPGA interface (no external SPI/FSMC bus), reducing PCB complexity and BOM part count. High-bandwidth on-chip AXI interconnect between CPU and FPGA fabric. ARM Cortex-A9 cores are substantially more powerful than Cortex-M7, capable of running Linux or bare-metal. Integrated DDR controller supports large data buffers. Lower total power than a discrete MCU+FPGA pair (~1.5-3 W typical for the full SoC).

Limitations: Higher BOM cost than MCU-only (∼€25-60 for the Zynq device). Requires both software (C/C++) and hardware (VHDL/Verilog) development expertise. Vivado toolchain is resource-intensive. BGA-only packages increase PCB manufacturing requirements. The ARM Cortex-A9 does not have deterministic interrupt response like a Cortex-M (may require the FPGA fabric for hard real-time tasks).

Option B: Embassy (Async Rust, Bare Metal)

Embassy is an async Rust framework for embedded systems that leverages Rust's async/await syntax and a cooperative executor to provide concurrent task management without the overhead of a preemptive scheduler.

Advantages: Embassy's async executor compiles to a state machine with no heap allocation and minimal context-switching overhead. Rust's ownership model eliminates entire classes of bugs (buffer overflows, use-after-free, data races) at compile time. High-quality async HAL drivers for STM32 (embassy-stm32) with native DMA, UART, SPI, I2C, ADC, and DAC support. Rust's strong type system is well-suited for AI-assisted development. Growing ecosystem supporting STM32, nRF, RP2040, and ESP32 families.

Limitations: Relatively young (~3 years), with a smaller deployed base and developer pool. Cooperative scheduling provides soft real-time guarantees only (deterministic with interrupt priority, but not preemptive). No safety certification (e.g., IEC 61508) available yet. DSP library support relies on FFI bindings (cmsis-dsp-sys) or Rust-native alternatives that may be less optimized. Debugging tooling (defmt, probe-rs) is good but less mature than GDB + SystemView.

CHOSEN

FreeRTOS — selected for its hard real-time guarantees, IEC 61508 certification availability, native STM32H743 integration, and the largest embedded developer ecosystem. Embassy remains a compelling alternative for future iterations where Rust's memory safety guarantees would reduce firmware defect rates.

/*===================================================================
 * ofdm_engine.c — OFDM Modulator / Demodulator
 *===================================================================*/
#include "ofdm_engine.h"
#include "bsp.h"
#include <math.h>

/* Pre-computed FFT twiddle factors (Q15 fixed-point) */
static int16_t twiddle_re[OFDM_NFFT];
static int16_t twiddle_im[OFDM_NFFT];

/* Pilot subcarrier indices: {7, 21, 43, 57} (802.11a-style) */
static const uint8_t pilot_idx[OFDM_NPILOT] = {7, 21, 43, 57};

/* QPSK constellation: {+1+1j, -1+1j, -1-1j, +1-1j} / sqrt(2) */
static const int16_t qpsk_I[4] = { 23170, -23170, -23170, 23170};
static const int16_t qpsk_Q[4] = { 23170, 23170, -23170, -23170};

void ofdm_init(void)
{
 for (uint16_t k = 0; k < OFDM_NFFT; k++) {
 float angle = -2.0f * M_PI * k / OFDM_NFFT;
 twiddle_re[k] = (int16_t)(cosf(angle) * 32767);
 twiddle_im[k] = (int16_t)(sinf(angle) * 32767);
 }
}

void ofdm_modulate(const uint8_t *bits, uint16_t nbits, ofdm_symbol_t *out)
{
 int16_t freq_I[OFDM_NFFT] = {0};
 int16_t freq_Q[OFDM_NFFT] = {0};
 uint16_t bpos = 0;

 /* Map bits → QPSK on data subcarriers */
 for (uint16_t k = 1; k < OFDM_NFFT / 2; k++) {
 bool is_pilot = false;
 for (uint8_t p = 0; p < OFDM_NPILOT; p++)
 if (pilot_idx[p] == k) { is_pilot = true; break; }

 if (is_pilot) {
 freq_I[k] = 32767; /* pilot = +1 */
 freq_Q[k] = 0;
 } else if (bpos + BITS_PER_SYM <= nbits) {
 uint8_t sym = (bits[bpos/8] >> (bpos % 8)) & 0x03;
 freq_I[k] = qpsk_I[sym];
 freq_Q[k] = qpsk_Q[sym];
 bpos += BITS_PER_SYM;
 }
 }

 /* IFFT (radix-2 DIT) → time-domain */
 bsp_ifft_q15(freq_I, freq_Q, out->I, out->Q, OFDM_NFFT);

 /* Prepend cyclic prefix (last NCP samples → front) */
 memmove(&out->I[OFDM_NCP], out->I, OFDM_NFFT * sizeof(int16_t));
 memmove(&out->Q[OFDM_NCP], out->Q, OFDM_NFFT * sizeof(int16_t));
 memcpy(out->I, &out->I[OFDM_NFFT], OFDM_NCP * sizeof(int16_t));
 memcpy(out->Q, &out->Q[OFDM_NFFT], OFDM_NCP * sizeof(int16_t));
}

int ofdm_demodulate(const rx_frame_t *in, uint8_t *bits, uint16_t max_bits)
{
 /* Strip CP, apply FFT, equalize, demap QPSK → bits */
 int16_t time_I[OFDM_NFFT], time_Q[OFDM_NFFT];
 int16_t freq_I[OFDM_NFFT], freq_Q[OFDM_NFFT];

 /* Skip CP: copy NFFT samples starting at offset NCP */
 for (uint16_t i = 0; i < OFDM_NFFT; i++) {
 time_I[i] = in->samples[(OFDM_NCP + i) * 2];
 time_Q[i] = in->samples[(OFDM_NCP + i) * 2 + 1];
 }

 bsp_fft_q15(time_I, time_Q, freq_I, freq_Q, OFDM_NFFT);

 /* QPSK demap: hard decision */
 uint16_t bpos = 0;
 memset(bits, 0, max_bits / 8 + 1);
 for (uint16_t k = 1; k < OFDM_NFFT / 2 && bpos + 2 <= max_bits; k++) {
 bool is_pilot = false;
 for (uint8_t p = 0; p < OFDM_NPILOT; p++)
 if (pilot_idx[p] == k) { is_pilot = true; break; }
 if (is_pilot) continue;

 uint8_t sym = ((freq_I[k] < 0) ? 1 : 0) | ((freq_Q[k] < 0) ? 2 : 0);
 bits[bpos/8] |= (sym << (bpos % 8));
 bpos += 2;
 }
 return bpos;
}

/*===================================================================
 * power_ctrl.c — Closed-Loop PI Power Controller
 * Loop rate : 10 kHz (100 µs period)
 * Method : Tustin (bilinear) discretization
 *===================================================================*/
#include "power_ctrl.h"
#include "bsp.h"

/* PI gains — tuned via Ziegler-Nichols on BVD plant model */
static float Kp = 0.8f;
static float Ki = 120.0f;
static const float Ts = 1.0f / MODEM_CTRL_HZ; /* 100 µs */
static const float SAT_HIGH = 1.0f;
static const float SAT_LOW = 0.0f;

/* State */
static float integral = 0.0f;
static float e_prev = 0.0f;
static float p_ref = 0.5f; /* normalised 0..1 */

void power_ctrl_init(void)
{
 integral = 0.0f;
 e_prev = 0.0f;
 bsp_adc_calibrate();
}

void power_ctrl_set_target(float p_ref_dBm)
{
 /* Convert dBm to normalised 0..1 */
 p_ref = powf(10.0f, (p_ref_dBm - 50.0f) / 10.0f);
 if (p_ref > 1.0f) p_ref = 1.0f;
 if (p_ref < 0.0f) p_ref = 0.0f;
}

static float pi_step(float error)
{
 /* Tustin integrator: I[n] = I[n-1] + (Ts/2)*(e[n] + e[n-1]) */
 integral += (Ts / 2.0f) * (error + e_prev) * Ki;
 e_prev = error;

 float u = Kp * error + integral;

 /* Anti-windup: clamp output & freeze integrator */
 if (u > SAT_HIGH) {
 u = SAT_HIGH;
 integral -= (Ts / 2.0f) * (error + e_prev) * Ki; /* back-calc */
 } else if (u < SAT_LOW) {
 u = SAT_LOW;
 integral -= (Ts / 2.0f) * (error + e_prev) * Ki;
 }
 return u;
}

{
 (void)arg;
 TickType_t wake = xTaskGetTickCount();
 const TickType_t period = pdMS_TO_TICKS(1000 / MODEM_CTRL_HZ);

 for (;;) {

③ Solution Planning

Multi-Level Simulation Approach

The modem design is validated through a four-level simulation hierarchy, progressing from abstract system models to hardware-in-the-loop testing:

Level 1: MATLAB/Simulink System Model

A floating-point behavioral model of the complete Tx-channel-Rx chain is implemented in MATLAB/Simulink. This model includes: OFDM modulator (IFFT + CP insertion), Butterworth BPF models (using butter and filter), underwater channel simulator (Bellhop ray tracer for multipath, Thorp absorption [8], Doppler shift), AWGN + colored noise generator (Urick model [7]), OFDM demodulator (CP removal + FFT + equalization), and BER/EVM measurement. This level validates the signal processing algorithms and establishes performance baselines (BER vs. SNR curves) for each modulation scheme [14].

Level 2: Python Discrete-Event Simulation

A Python-based DES (using SimPy) models the RTOS task scheduling, queue congestion, and real-time deadlines. Scenarios include: baseline operation (nominal throughput, latency), multi-range sweep (varying distance to characterize BER degradation), fault injection (task overruns, queue overflow, power faults), and stress testing (maximum packet rate, concurrent Tx/Rx). This validates the software architecture before committing to firmware implementation.

Level 3: LTspice Analog Circuit Simulation

The analog front-end (BPF, PA, LNA, AGC) is simulated in LTspice with real component models. Key analyses: AC sweep (Bode plot verification), transient response (step and impulse), THD analysis at full power, noise figure simulation for the Rx chain, and Monte Carlo tolerance analysis for filter component variations.

Level 4: Bellhop Channel Propagation

The Bellhop Gaussian beam tracer [1] is used to generate realistic multipath channel impulse responses (CIRs) for different deployment scenarios. Input parameters include bathymetry, sound speed profile (from CTD measurements or the Mackenzie equation [9]), surface roughness, and bottom type. The CIRs are imported into the MATLAB model to validate performance under realistic propagation conditions.

The IP68 rating certifies the enclosure against dust ingress (6 = totally dust-tight) and water immersion (8 = continuous immersion beyond 1 m, conditions specified by manufacturer). For our 300 m depth rating, the enclosure must withstand 30 bar hydrostatic pressure continuously. Testing involves:

  • Dust test: 8 hours in a dust chamber with circulating talcum powder.
  • Pressure test: Sustained immersion at 300 m equivalent pressure (31 bar) for a minimum of 4 hours, followed by functional verification.
  • Connector seal test: All SubConn or similar connectors tested to rated pressure independently.

Estimated cost: €3,000-8,000 (testing + certification). Timeline: 2-3 weeks.

3. IEC 60945 — Maritime Navigation and Radiocommunication Equipment

IEC 60945 is the general requirements standard for maritime electronic equipment. It covers: environmental testing (temperature: −15 to +55°C, humidity: 93% RH, vibration, salt mist), EMC requirements specific to shipboard environments (more stringent than general CE for some parameters), electrical safety (insulation, grounding, surge protection), and compass safe distance. This standard is particularly relevant if the modem is installed on vessels.

Estimated cost: €8,000-20,000 (comprehensive environmental + EMC testing). Timeline: 4-8 weeks.

This is the successor to IEC 60065 and IEC 60950-1, covering electrical safety for ICT equipment. Conclusion

This report has presented a comprehensive design for an underwater acoustic OFDM modem meeting the specified requirements of 10 kbps net data rate, 100 W transmit power, and −100 dBm(A) receiver sensitivity in the 20-40 kHz band. The key design decisions are:

  • Analog front-end: Butterworth bandpass filters (4th-order Tx, 6th-order Rx) providing maximally flat passband response suitable for OFDM subcarrier uniformity.
  • OFDM parameters: 64-point FFT, 48 data subcarriers, 312.5 Hz spacing, 25% cyclic prefix, yielding 4 ms symbol duration and robust multipath tolerance.
  • Modulation: QPSK as the optimal balance of spectral efficiency and robustness, supported by extensive literature evidence from deployed underwater systems.
  • Transducer control: BVD RLC model with closed-loop digital PI controller (Tustin discretization, anti-windup) at 10 kHz update rate.
  • Architecture: STM32H743 MCU-only platform (with ULX3S (ECP5 + ESP32) as a rapid-prototyping and educational alternative), running FreeRTOS with 5 tasks at ~10% CPU utilization.
  • Firmware: Modular C skeleton with 11 source files, layered BSP/middleware/application architecture, and clean IPC via queues, mutexes, and event groups.
  • Validation: Four-level simulation (MATLAB, Python DES, LTspice, Bellhop) followed by V-model testing culminating in sea trials.
  • Certification: Comprehensive regulatory pathway covering CE, IP68, IEC 60945, and optional DNV-GL type approval, estimated at €18-98K over 3-12 months.

The design provides significant margins in computational performance, power budget, and memory utilization, ensuring headroom for future enhancements such as adaptive modulation, higher-order FEC, and multi-element beamforming.
